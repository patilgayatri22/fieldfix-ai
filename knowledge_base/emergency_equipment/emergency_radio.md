# Emergency Radio — Repair Guide (Hand-Crank / Battery / NOAA Weather Radio)

## Common Symptoms
- Radio receives no stations or just static
- Weak or no sound from speaker
- Hand-crank doesn't charge internal battery
- NOAA weather alerts not received
- Display shows but no audio
- Radio works briefly then cuts out
- AM works but FM doesn't (or vice versa)
- Solar panel not charging

## Likely Causes
- No reception → no antenna (collapsed), wrong band selected, no signal in area
- Weak sound → low battery, volume at minimum, speaker damaged
- Hand-crank not charging → internal battery fully dead (won't accept charge), charging circuit fault, worn crank gear
- NOAA not receiving → SAME code programmed incorrectly, NOAA band not selected, weak signal
- Display but no audio → mute activated, volume control broken, speaker wire disconnected
- Cuts out → battery nearly depleted, thermal issue, power switch intermittent
- One band missing → band switch dirty/worn, internal tuning module fault
- Solar not charging → solar panel dirty, insufficient light, depleted battery not accepting trickle charge

## Safe First Checks
- Check and extend antenna fully — telescoping antennas must be fully extended for best reception
- Check volume control — turn up fully
- Check battery level indicator or measure battery with multimeter if accessible
- Try plugging in USB charge cable if supported — bypasses internal battery to confirm radio works
- For NOAA: confirm NOAA/weather band is selected (separate button or position on band switch)
- Check mute button hasn't been accidentally pressed

## Tools Needed
- Multimeter
- USB charge cable (if radio supports USB charging)
- Replacement AA/AAA batteries (if battery-powered model)
- Soft cloth (for solar panel cleaning)

## Step-by-Step Guidance
1. Extend antenna to full length. Orient in different directions while tuning.
2. Check battery charge level. For AA/AAA models → replace batteries. For rechargeable internal battery → connect USB charger for at least 2 hours before testing.
3. If USB charging restores function → internal battery was depleted. Charge for 4–8 hours to fully restore.
4. For hand-crank not charging → crank must generate enough speed: 1–2 minutes of fast cranking typically provides only 5–10 minutes of radio use. This is normal — hand-crank is for emergency use only, not primary charging.
5. If cranking feels free (no resistance) → crank gear stripped. Cannot be field-repaired. Use USB or battery as backup.
6. For poor AM reception → AM antennas are inside the radio (ferrite bar antenna, directional). Rotate the radio body — AM signals are directional.
7. For poor FM reception → extend telescoping antenna fully and angle at 45°. Move away from metal objects which block FM.
8. For NOAA not alerting → enter settings menu. Program your area's SAME code (from noaa.gov or weather service). Without correct SAME code, specific regional alerts won't trigger.
9. For solar panel not charging → clean panel surface with soft damp cloth. Solar charging requires direct bright sunlight — not indoor lighting. Shade or overcast reduces output 70–90%.
10. If radio works on USB/AC but not internal battery → internal battery has failed. For models with accessible battery → replace with equivalent NiMH battery pack.

## Stop Conditions
- **STOP** attempting to open sealed radio unit to access internal battery — warranty void, risk of damage
- Do not expose radio to direct rain unless rated waterproof — most emergency radios are splash-resistant only
- **STOP** if radio produces burning smell or excessive heat — disconnect power source immediately

## Prevention
- Test emergency radio monthly — tune to NOAA weather service to verify reception
- Partially recharge internal battery every 3 months even when not in use — deep discharge kills NiMH cells
- Store radio with antenna collapsed to prevent damage
- Keep solar panel surface clean — even light dust reduces output significantly
- Verify NOAA SAME code is programmed for your county/region
